The Old Telling

The hill folk had a name for her: the Jinroot Fox.

She was no common animal, they said — the root's own shadow, sent up from the dark to test whoever came digging. The greedy she led in circles until dark. The patient she led home. Trappers swore they'd see her at first light on the ridgeline, then gone the moment they blinked.

The Bargain of the Slope

Take only the four-prong. Leave the berries. Speak your thanks.

That was the bargain, passed from one sang-hunter to the next. Break it — dig the young root, take the seed, leave no thanks — and the fox would not run for you again. The slope would close its oldest roots to you forever. Keep it, and she'd lead you to the proof your grandfather only dreamed of.

The Root That Listens

They said the oldest root could hear you coming.

A root that has weathered two hundred winters learns the sound of boots and the sound of greed. The old diggers walked soft and spoke soft. “The root don't lie,” they'd say, “you just gotta be quiet enough to hear it.” The fox was only there to teach you how to listen.

A Hunter Named HEZ

Most chased her for the money. HEZ followed to listen.

A third-generation digger, he walked the Bourbon County ridges before light for thirty seasons, reading the land the way the old tellings taught. He kept the Code while others stripped the hills bare. And on one cold morning she stopped, looked back once, and showed him the root older than the county itself.

The Proof in the Bottle

He carried the root.
We carry the proof.

The legend isn't decoration — it's the recipe. Every bottle of Ancient Proof keeps the Code the fox taught: take only what's earned, give thanks, and let time do the rest.
